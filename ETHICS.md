# Ethics — Seedlight

*This document does not have answers. It has honest questions and the principles we use to navigate them.*

---

## Why This Document Exists

Most technology projects write ethics documents after someone raises a concern. We are writing this one first, because the concerns here are not edge cases — they are central to what Seedlight is trying to do.

If you are considering contributing to Seedlight, read this document before anything else. These are not disclaimers. They are the load-bearing walls of the project.

---

## Tension 1 — Who Decides the Direction?

Seedlight proposes to guide children toward futures. This immediately raises the most important question of the entire project: **who has the right to decide what future a child should be guided toward?**

We reject the extremes on both ends.

The authoritarian model — where the state, the system, or an algorithm decides a child's path — produces conformity and suppresses human potential. History is full of examples of what happens when institutions decide who people should become.

The pure autonomy model — where the child decides everything from the beginning — ignores the reality that children cannot meaningfully choose from possibilities they have never been exposed to. A child cannot choose to become an astrophysicist if she has never heard the word "star" in a scientific context.

Our current working principle is this: **Seedlight should expand the range of possibilities a child encounters, never narrow it.** The system should open doors, not close them. It should present futures, not prescribe them. The child — and eventually the adult they become — makes the real choices. The system only ensures those choices are made from the widest possible horizon.

This principle sounds simple. Implementing it is not. We hold it openly as an ongoing design constraint, not a solved problem.

## Tension 2 — Predictive Equity vs. Predictive Discrimination

The most powerful and most dangerous feature of Seedlight is predictive equity — the idea of proactively preparing children from underserved communities for emerging opportunities.

The risk is real and must be named directly: **any system that uses historical data to predict futures for children will inherit the biases of that data.**

If the data shows that children from certain communities historically end up in certain types of work, a naive algorithm will prepare those children for more of the same work. This is not equity. This is automation of injustice with the aesthetic of progress.

Predictive equity only works if it is actively anti-historical in its design. It must ask not "what have people like this child historically become?" but "what could this child become if history were not a constraint?"

This requires deliberate, auditable mechanisms to detect and counteract bias in the prediction models. It requires diverse human oversight. It requires the humility to acknowledge that no algorithm can fully escape the data it was trained on — and to build corrective processes around that limitation.

We commit to publishing and maintaining a public bias audit for any predictive models Seedlight develops.

## Tension 3 — The Data of Children

A longitudinal companion that builds a rich cognitive and behavioral map of a child over years is, by definition, a system that holds sensitive data about a minor.

This is not a small concern. It is potentially the most consequential design decision in the entire project.

Our principles on this are non-negotiable:

**Data sovereignty belongs to the learner.** The cognitive map built by Seedlight belongs to the child — and when they become an adult, to them alone. No institution, no government, no company should have access to this data without explicit, informed consent.

**The system must be survivable.** If Seedlight as an organization ceases to exist, the data of every child in the system must either transfer to the learner directly or be deleted. There is no acceptable version where a company's bankruptcy means a child's learning history disappears or, worse, gets sold.

**Minimalism by default.** The system should collect only what is necessary for the pedagogical function. Not what is useful for marketing, not what might be valuable someday, not what investors might find interesting. Only what serves the child's learning.

**No behavioral advertising, ever.** The data generated by a child learning is not a product. We commit permanently to this principle, regardless of funding pressures.

## Tension 4 — The Teacher Question

We have stated that Seedlight is not a replacement for teachers. We hold this principle genuinely. But we also need to be honest about a risk that exists regardless of our intentions.

Tools that make education more efficient without corresponding investment in teachers create economic and political pressure to reduce teaching staff. This has happened before. It will happen again. The fact that we don't intend this outcome does not mean we are not responsible for creating conditions that enable it.

Our commitment: Seedlight will actively oppose its own misuse. If we observe or receive evidence that Seedlight is being deployed as a justification to defund teaching, we will speak publicly against that use. We will build into the design explicit indicators that flag when teacher-student ratios fall below thresholds that research identifies as harmful. We will partner with educator organizations, not route around them.

---

## Tension 5 — Who Builds This?

Open source projects are not neutral. They reflect the values, assumptions, and blind spots of the people who build them.

A project built exclusively by technologists will produce a technologist's vision of education. A project built exclusively in wealthy countries will produce a vision of education shaped by wealthy-country assumptions.

We commit to actively seeking voices from communities that are most affected by educational inequality — not as consultants, not as users, but as co-designers with genuine authority over the direction of the project.

This is harder than it sounds. It requires resources, translation, cultural navigation, and the willingness to have the project's direction genuinely changed by perspectives that challenge its current assumptions. We are committing to that difficulty.

---

## What We Will Not Do

These are absolute commitments, not guidelines:

- We will not sell or license learner data to third parties.
- We will not build features designed to maximize engagement at the expense of learning quality.
- We will not allow predictive models to narrow a child's perceived possibilities.
- We will not accept funding from sources whose interests conflict with learner sovereignty.
- We will not deploy Seedlight in a context where we have evidence it is being used to replace rather than support human educators without publicly opposing that use.

---

## An Invitation

This document will be wrong in ways we cannot currently see. We are asking the community to find those ways and bring them to us.

Open an issue. Challenge a principle. Propose a better framework. The ethics of Seedlight are not finished — they are in permanent revision, held accountable by everyone who cares enough to engage.

That accountability is not a burden. It is the point.
